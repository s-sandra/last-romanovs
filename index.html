NaNoGenMo

National Novel Generation Month

What is NNGM?

In 2013, internet artist Darius Kazemi decided to participate in National Novel Writing Month, but instead of writing a fifty-thousand word novel from scratch, he created a program to do it for him. Thus was born Teens Wander Around a House, which he made by having AI agents wander through a simulation, narrate their actions and pull conversations from Twitter. Kazemi's idea quickly caught on, and NaNoGenMo (National Novel Generation Month) continues as an annual event in November, where participants create programs that write fifty thousand word novels. For six years, the coding celebration has brought together a community of artists and programmers from around the world, from beginners to prolific creative coders such as Nick Montfort, Liza Daly, Allison Parrish and Martin O'Leary.

Hey, who wants to join me in NaNoGenMo: spend the month writing code that generates a 50k word novel, share the novel & the code at the end -Darius Kazemi (@tinysubversions) November 1, 2013

How to Participate

NNGM moderators start reviewing entries on November 1 of each year, and accept them as late as the first week of December. To enter, simply open an issue in the NaNoGenMo GitHub repository for the current year (the 2020 repo will be available in November). Some create an issue declaring an intent to participate, others use their issue as a dev diary, or to request help or feedback from the NNGM community. When your program is finished, place the link to your source code and a preview or description of your content as a comment in your issue. The rules are simple. For an entry to be marked with a completed tag, the code must have been written in November and it must produce at least fifty-thousand words.

Note that your NNGM source code does not have to be hosted on GitHub. Past entrants have placed their code in tweets (for Nano-NaNoGenMo, where the goal is to write a program under 250 characters), on their websites or in another hosting service, like GitLab or Bitbucket.

Why Study NNGM?

The study of computer-generated literature, which frequently appropriates text from known sources, allows researchers to glean insight into the influences and mechanisms of contemporary language and question notions of authorship, creative expression and artificial intelligence. Looking at these works allows us to identify patterns and trends within entry genres and text generation methods.

One such trend is the use of literary classics as a foundation for generative novels - creators build upon themes and qualities of these works, often revealing new meanings within these familiar pieces through code and generative creativity. Common works appropriated include Moby Dick, Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland, The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes, and multiple versions of the Bible, along with the works of William Shakespeare, H.P. Lovecraft, and Jane Austen - particularly Pride and Prejudice. NaNoGenMo participants also take inspiration in a wide variety of fields of study, from linguistics, culture, and music to geography, visual art, and computer science.

Cristina Montemorano, fellow NNGMC documenter

Other trends include common methods of generation which has led to the emergence and evolution of NaNoGenMo-specific “genres.” For instance, these nascent genres include a trend toward visual art or concretism - automating the generation of ASCII art - and a kind of procedurally-generated narratives wherein a program simulates a series of circumstances and then attempts to describe the experience of travelling through them. These “genres” are notable not only because they create unique work, but also because they aid in visualizing the process through which programs create work, one which differs fundamentally from that of traditional human authors.

Alex Rudenshiold, fellow NNGMC documenter

From Dada to Java

As digital technology occupies or gives shape to every facet of our daily lives, it is inevitable that computers will be part of how we imagine our future and think about the world around us. Computer-generated fiction offers us many ways to conceptualize the creative potentials of artificial intelligence, experiment with avant-garde poetics, and imagine the futures of literature in the 21st century.

Zach Whalen, NNGMC founder
